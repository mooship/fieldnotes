Home is not something most of us have to define. It's the place that exists when you're not in it — that accumulates your presence, holds your history, keeps the people who know you. You can lose it in many ways. Through war, poverty, time, displacement. But most of those losses, however devastating, leave the question of return at least theoretically open. The structure is gone. The possibility, maybe, isn't.

It is worth asking what it looks like when the erasure of that possibility is the point.

The EU and UN have assessed that Gaza's development has been set back 77 years. 1948 was 77 years ago. That number deserves a moment — not because the symmetry is coincidental, but because it describes a destination.

---

Since October 7, 2023, 72,599 Palestinians have been killed in Gaza. Over 172,000 more have been injured. 1.9 million people have been displaced, many repeatedly. 60% of the population is currently homeless. These are the figures as of late April 2026, and they continue to move.

But the death toll, as significant as it is, understates the scope of what has happened. 90% of Gaza's water and sanitation infrastructure has been destroyed. 371,000 housing units have been destroyed or damaged. Hospitals, universities, archives, water treatment plants — not as collateral damage but as consistent targets in a pattern that holds across the entire duration of the campaign. Rubble at this scale, with this targeting logic, functions as something beyond the immediate catastrophe. It is a prevention of return even after the shooting stops. You cannot go home to a place that cannot support life.

The 77-year assessment names what has been done in infrastructural terms. That is not a figure about the past. It is a figure about the future — about how long it would take, under sustained reconstruction effort, to restore what existed before. Those conditions do not currently exist.

But destruction is one method. Another is simply taking the land.

---

Since March 2026, multiple Israeli divisions have been operating in southern Lebanon up to the Litani River — approximately one-tenth of Lebanon's territory under effective military control. The Litani has been a line before, in 1978 and again in 1982. It is not a new boundary in the imagination of Israeli military and political planning.

What is more explicit now is the political intention. Finance Minister Smotrich and Defence Minister Katz have named annexation of southern Lebanon as a goal — not as a fringe position, but as stated policy from senior cabinet members. Over 1,200 people have been killed in Lebanon. More than a million have been displaced.

The method here differs from Gaza: the land is not being made uninhabitable. It is being claimed. Military presence converting to territorial control, control converting — if the political project advances — to permanent annexation. In February 2026, 19 countries formally condemned de facto annexation moves. The 2024 International Court of Justice advisory opinion had already found Israel's presence in the Occupied Palestinian Territories to be unlawful under international law. These condemnations exist. They are real. They have not slowed the process.

And then there is the quietest method — the one that requires no bombs at all.

---

In February 2026, the Israeli cabinet transferred authority over land registration in the West Bank from military administration to the civilian Ministry of Justice. The change appears administrative. Its effects are not.

Land previously held under military administration was at least nominally subject to international humanitarian law — the legal framework governing occupied territory. Under civilian authority, the mechanism for classifying land as "state property," and therefore available for confiscation and settlement, becomes faster and harder to challenge. Any land where Palestinians cannot demonstrate formal ownership through Israeli-recognised documentation becomes, by this logic, Israel's to allocate.

The settlement numbers give the scale: over 36,000 housing units advanced or approved in the West Bank and East Jerusalem in the current period. 84 new outposts established at an unprecedented rate. Over 36,000 Palestinians forcibly displaced alongside this expansion.

Home is not only a structure. It is the accumulation of being-in-a-place over time: title, memory, community, the relationship to specific land. Settlement expansion and administrative confiscation do not merely remove people from where they are. They overwrite the record of their presence. This is the most patient of the three methods. It requires no dramatic event — only forms, decisions, reclassifications. Each individual step can be described as procedural.

Three methods. One destination.

---

Gaza is the military operation. Lebanon is the territorial expansion. The West Bank is the administrative consolidation. These are not three separate crises generating three separate responses. They are concurrent, with the same political beneficiaries and the same political logic.

The International Court of Justice found in 2024 that Israel's presence in the Occupied Palestinian Territories is unlawful under international law. This is not a contested legal question. The world's highest court has answered it. Nineteen governments have formally condemned what is happening in the West Bank. UN experts have warned that global impunity is the mechanism enabling the violations to continue. The ritual of condemnation is real — resolutions passed, statements issued, advisories published. The legal and normative framework exists.

What it is not is a constraint.

This is the hardest part to sit with — not the violence, which is visible and documented, but the architecture of response to it. The world built an elaborate structure of international law and multilateral institutions precisely to prevent this: territorial acquisition by force, systematic dispossession, the erasure of a people's claim to exist in a place. That structure has issued its verdict. The project continues.

The Nakba was a specific event — 1948, the displacement of approximately 700,000 Palestinians. It was also, retroactively, a beginning. What is being documented now, across Gaza, Lebanon, and the West Bank, is whether that project reaches a conclusion it cannot be walked back from.

Not the homes. The possibility of home.
